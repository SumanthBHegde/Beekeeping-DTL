# Scope Decision

## Why we needed scope decisions

The problem space of beekeeping is vast, encompassing complex biology, agricultural economics, physical hardware, and market supply chains. Because we cannot solve the entire spectrum of apiculture problems within a single semester's timeframe, we must deliberately narrow our focus. Making these scope decisions explicit ensures that our Ideation and Prototyping phases remain realistic, achievable, and tightly aligned with our core problem statement.

## Constraints

- **Time constraints:** The limited project duration restricts us from conducting long-term biological testing, multi-season observation, or longitudinal yield tracking.
- **Technical constraints:** We have limitations around advanced manufacturing and electronics, focusing our team on low-tech, mechanical, or retro-fitted hardware solutions rather than complex IoT/AI systems.
- **Geographic criteria:** Our environment context is strictly constrained to the local climate, flora, and traditional practices found in the Sirsi/Karnataka region.
- **User group focus:** We are explicitly designing for small-scale traditional farmers and enthusiastic beginners with strict budget limitations, not large commercial or industrial operators.

## In-scope

- Safer, simpler, and non-disruptive routine hive inspection methods.
- Tools or workflows designed specifically to reduce perceived risk and the fear of stings for novices.
- Low-cost, modular mechanical add-ons that retrofit onto existing traditional wooden boxes (under ₹4,000).
- External or closed-loop feeding mechanisms to prevent internal temperature drops.
- Basic, intuitive awareness features that help users understand hive health.

## Out-of-scope

- Fully automated, AI-driven, or IoT-based disease diagnosis and colony monitoring.
- Complete top-to-bottom redesigns of industrial-scale apiary operations.
- Building direct-to-consumer software platforms or national-level honey marketing supply chains.
- Attempting long-term government policy, subsidy, or regulatory changes.
- Developing high-cost luxury systems (like the ₹25,000 Flow Hive).

## Success criteria

- Farmers and beginners report a significantly lower perceived risk of stings when using the proposed solution.
- The physical effort or number of structural steps required for routine feeding and inspection is demonstrably reduced.
- The solution does not trigger aggressive swarming or massive heat loss during operation.
- Users verify that the solution is intuitive and understandable without constant supervision from a society expert.
- The theoretical manufacturing cost meets the strict economic reality of local agriculture.

## Impact on next phases

By drawing these clear boundaries, our brainstorming sessions in [03-ideate/](../03-ideate/README.md) will stay focused—we will not waste time generating out-of-scope ideas like expensive digital apps or entirely new commercial supply chains. Likewise, as we build physical answers in the [04-prototype/](../04-prototype/README.md) phase, every iteration will be evaluated directly against the success criteria listed above to ensure we are actually solving the right problem.
