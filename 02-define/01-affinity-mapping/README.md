# Affinity Mapping

## What affinity mapping means in this project

Affinity mapping was the step where we took raw post-it-style notes from interviews, survey responses, field observations, and quotes, then grouped them by similarity. The goal was to move from many scattered observations to a small set of clear patterns that explain the real problems beekeepers and consumers face.

## Process followed

1. Collected individual insights from empathy research and wrote each one as a separate note.
2. Grouped similar notes together silently, based on recurring ideas like fear, time, cost, and hive health.
3. Named each cluster with a clear theme so the group reflected the underlying issue, not just a single comment.
4. Reviewed the clusters as a team and refined them until the themes felt consistent and non-overlapping.

## Theme clusters

### Theme 1: Perceived risk and fear

- Fear of bee stings appeared repeatedly, especially among beginners who avoided opening the hive unless absolutely necessary.
- Several interviews showed that people were nervous about disturbing the colony because it could trigger aggressive behavior.
- One common pattern was the feeling that beekeeping is dangerous without proper handling knowledge or protective confidence.

Representative notes:

- "My biggest fear is getting stung." [Sushanth Hegde interview]
- "If I open the box, they will attack." [Beginner interview]
- Fear of sting risk was one of the strongest barriers in both interviews and survey-style feedback.

### Theme 2: Knowledge and training gap

- Beginners often said they did not know how to diagnose colony health, pests, or disease early enough.
- Interviewees described learning by trial and error, which led to losses when the colony absconded or became weak.
- There was a repeated need for simple guidance, especially for identifying problems without fully opening the box.

Representative notes:

- Lack of training was linked to colony loss and beginner dropout.
- Several respondents asked for easier ways to inspect hive health.
- Knowledge gaps showed up in questions about disease detection, feeder use, and maintenance timing.

### Theme 3: Time and workload

- Beekeeping was described as physically demanding because boxes need shifting, feeding, and seasonal management.
- Opening the hive for routine feeding or checking was seen as time-consuming and disruptive.
- Farmers wanted methods that reduced repeated manual work, especially during hot weather or monsoon season.

Representative notes:

- Seasonal migration of boxes was a recurring maintenance task.
- Manual feeding and inspection added extra labor during already busy farming periods.
- People preferred solutions that reduced the number of times the hive had to be opened.

### Theme 4: Cost and equipment

- Many notes pointed to the high cost of imported or advanced systems as a major blocker.
- Farmers consistently compared expensive products with their limited yearly honey output and local budgets.
- There was strong interest in low-cost, modular, retrofit-friendly equipment instead of full replacement systems.

Representative notes:

- Flow Hive pricing was repeatedly described as unrealistic for local farmers.
- Small-scale beekeepers favored durable tools that could fit existing boxes.
- Cost-effectiveness was treated as a non-negotiable requirement, not a nice-to-have.

### Theme 5: Market and trust in honey

- Notes from interviews and consumer feedback showed a trust gap around honey purity and direct sourcing.
- Farmers felt they were forced into bulk sales, while end-buyers were willing to pay more for verified quality.
- There was also interest in value-added bee products, but limited technology and training to support them.

Representative notes:

- Consumers wanted pure, unadulterated honey from a known source.
- Farmers talked about the gap between bulk sale prices and retail potential.
- By-products such as royal jelly and pollen were seen as underused opportunities.

## Key synthesis takeaways

- Fear and lack of confidence appear in almost every beginner-focused conversation.
- Knowledge gaps are not just about theory; they directly lead to colony loss, poor maintenance, and hesitation to scale.
- Time and labor pressures make frequent hive opening a practical problem, not just an inconvenience.
- Cost is a deciding factor for adoption, and any solution that is too expensive will be rejected immediately.
- Trust matters on both sides: farmers want better market value, and consumers want proof of purity and authenticity.
