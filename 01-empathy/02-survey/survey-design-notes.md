# Survey Design Notes

Design decisions and sampling notes for the survey.

## Questionnaire Design Rationale

The questionnaire for bee farmers was structured in three sections to gather practical and behavioral data that informs hive-box design and understanding of beekeeper needs.

### Section A: Basic Profile

**Why these questions matter:**

1. **Experience level (Q1)**: Understanding how long a beekeeper has been in the profession helps us understand training needs and willingness to adopt new tools. Newer beekeepers may have different pain points than experienced ones; older beekeepers may resist innovation.

2. **Number of colonies (Q2)**: Scale of operation determines economic viability and infrastructure needs. Small-scale beekeepers (1-5 hives) have different constraints than commercial operations; they need affordable, labor-efficient solutions.

3. **Purpose of beekeeping (Q3)**: A beekeeper who focuses on pollination services may have different harvesting needs than one focused on honey production. Income streams vary by purpose.

4. **Migratory beekeeping (Q4)**: Migration is mentioned repeatedly in the research as a major constraint. Knowing who migrates helps us understand the need for portable, durable, easy-to-transport hive designs.

### Section B: Hive Management

**Why these questions matter:**

5. **Inspection frequency (Q5)**: Regular inspection is essential for disease monitoring and colony health. This establishes baseline practice and shows how often beekeepers interact with their hives. A hive-box design must support frequent access without causing excessive disruption.

6. **Difficulty opening/inspecting (Q6)**: This directly targets the pain point. Physical difficulty during inspection signals a design opportunity—easier, less strenuous access mechanisms could reduce labor burden.

7. **Bee disturbance during inspection (Q7)**: Tests awareness of the impact of inspection on colony health. A good hive design should minimize colony disruption. This question establishes whether beekeepers see disturbance as a concern.

8. **Most difficult part of management (Q8)**: Open-ended ranking of challenges. Honey extraction is listed as a specific option because the research shows conventional extraction is labor-intensive and requires expensive equipment. This question helps prioritize which aspect of hive management to improve.

9. **Use of modern tools (Q9-Q10)**: Establishes current adoption of technology. India still has low adoption of scientific practices according to the research. Understanding what tools are already in use shows gaps and opportunities for new solutions that fit existing workflows.

### Section C: Problems and Challenges

**Why these questions matter:**

11. **Colony losses to pests/disease (Q11)**: Pests and diseases are the #1 constraint mentioned in both the Maharashtra and Haryana studies (86.7% of respondents listed this). Understanding frequency of losses shows the scale of the problem and urgency of better management solutions.

12. **Pesticide exposure (Q12)**: Pesticide poisoning was listed as a major constraint by 58% of respondents in the Haryana study. This question tests whether pesticide pressure affects the local context.

13. **Knowledge of bee health (Q13)**: Tests whether lack of knowledge is a barrier. Combined with Q14, this shows the gap between formal training and informal learning.

14. **Formal training received (Q14)**: The research shows that beekeepers learn through a mix of formal training and peer learning. Understanding who has formal training reveals training accessibility and the role that new tools must play in reducing knowledge barriers.

15. **Biggest challenge (Q15)**: Asks beekeepers to rank their top challenges (low yield, bee mortality, training gaps, equipment cost, etc.). This prioritizes which problems the project should address.

## Survey Administration Notes

- **Language**: The questionnaire is bilingual (English and Kannada) to improve accessibility for rural beekeepers.
- **Format**: Can be administered on paper or digitally (Google Forms, etc.).
- **Target sample**: Small and marginal beekeepers (primary users for the hive-box design project).

## How responses inform the design project

- Questions about inspection and disturbance guide requirements for easy-open, low-stress hive designs.
- Questions about challenges and modern tool adoption show where automation or simplification can add value.
- Experience level and scale of operation inform whether the design should be scalable or optimized for small operators.
- Migration patterns indicate whether the hive must be portable and durable during transport.

*(Note: These design questions map directly back to the secondary research findings regarding pests, physical harvest strain, and migration.)*
